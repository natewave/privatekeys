# Chapter 1 — The Notary

The office smelled of old paper and radiator heat. Nael sat in a leather chair that had been repaired at least twice — he could feel the seams through his jeans — and watched the notary's hands move across the desk. Maître Aubert. The name was embossed on a brass plate that caught the fluorescent light whenever the man shifted. He had not offered coffee.

"Monsieur Kadiri, as I mentioned on the phone, your uncle designated you as his sole heir."

Nael nodded. He had taken the afternoon off work for this. His manager had asked if someone died and Nael had said yes, and his manager had said *oh* in the particular tone people use when they realize a casual question has landed somewhere real. Nael had told him it was fine. It mostly was.

"The estate is modest," Maître Aubert continued, turning a page. He wore reading glasses that sat too low on his nose, and he had the habit of looking over them as if everything in the room mildly disappointed him. "No real property. No active bank accounts of significant value. There is a small sum — four hundred and twelve euros — in a Société Générale account that will be transferred to you after processing fees."

Four hundred and twelve euros. Nael almost smiled. His uncle Tarik had lived in — how many countries? He'd lost count of the postmarks. And this was the sum total. A number that wouldn't cover a month's rent in Paris.

"There is, however, a box of personal effects." The notary gestured toward a cardboard box on the side table. It was smaller than Nael expected. Roughly the size of a shoebox, sealed with clear packing tape that had begun to yellow. "These were recovered from a storage unit in Montreuil. The lease was paid through the end of this year."

"A storage unit," Nael repeated.

"Correct. The lease will not be renewed. I would advise you to inspect the unit before the deadline, in case there are additional items. The address is in the documentation."

Maître Aubert slid a folder across the desk — a thin one, barely worth the cardboard it was printed on. Nael opened it. A death certificate. A photocopy of an old Moroccan passport. A single-page will, handwritten, dated eighteen months ago. The handwriting was neat, slanted to the right, and entirely unfamiliar. He realized he had never seen his uncle's writing before.

The will was three sentences long:

*I, Tarik Kadiri, being of sound mind, leave all my possessions to my nephew Nael Kadiri. He will know what to do with them. I trust him with this, as I should have trusted him with more.*

Nael read it twice. The last sentence sat in his chest like a stone he hadn't swallowed.

"Do you have any questions, Monsieur Kadiri?"

He had a thousand. None of them were for the notary.

"No," he said. "Thank you."

---

The box rode on the passenger seat of the Métro, balanced on Nael's knees because there was nowhere else to put it. Line 9, Opéra to Oberkampf. A Wednesday afternoon, the car half-empty. An old woman across from him glanced at the box and then at his face, as though trying to calculate whether it held something fragile. Nael looked out the window at the tunnel walls strobing past and thought about the last time he'd seen Tarik.

He'd been seven. Maybe eight. Tarik had come to the apartment in Vitry-sur-Seine, the one with the yellow kitchen and the radiators that clanked at night. Nael's mother had opened the door and stood very still for a moment before stepping aside. Tarik was tall — that was the clearest memory — tall and wearing a coat that smelled like somewhere else. He'd brought a gift wrapped in newspaper: a small brass compass with Arabic script on the dial. Nael still had it somewhere. A drawer, a box, a half-forgotten corner.

They had spoken for less than an hour. Nael remembered his mother's voice through the bedroom wall — not the words, just the pitch. Rising. Controlled. Tarik had left before dinner.

After that: postcards. A birthday card from Cairo, three months late. A photograph of a desert with no note attached. Then nothing for years, until Nael's mother mentioned, in the way she mentioned everything about Tarik, almost accidentally, that he was "somewhere in South America." And then nothing again.

And now this. A box. A will. Four hundred and twelve euros.

---

His apartment was on the fourth floor, no elevator. Two rooms plus a kitchen you could cross in three steps. The desk faced the window and the window faced a courtyard where someone was always smoking. Nael set the box on the desk, next to his laptop and a coffee mug he'd been reusing for four days, and stood looking at it.

He peeled the tape slowly. Inside, the items had been packed with care — each wrapped in a sheet of newspaper, *Le Monde*, issues from months he didn't check. He laid them out on the desk one by one.

A leather notebook. Small, palm-sized, the cover darkened with age and handling. He opened it. Pages of tight handwriting — the same rightward slant as the will — but this was different. Numbers. Symbols. Words in French, Arabic, English, sometimes in the same sentence. Diagrams that looked like circuit schematics or maps or both. He turned the pages and understood nothing.

A set of photographs. Seven in total, printed on matte paper. A market at dusk, lanterns strung between buildings. A mountain with snow on its peak and nothing around it. A woman's hands holding a clay bowl — no face, just the hands. A door painted blue, set into a white wall. The others were landscapes he couldn't place. No writing on the backs.

And at the bottom of the box, wrapped more carefully than the rest in a square of soft cloth, a USB drive.

It was heavier than a normal flash drive — machined aluminum, matte black, no logo. One end terminated in a standard connector; the other was capped with a threaded metal sleeve, the kind designed to survive a house fire. Nael turned it between his fingers. It looked like something built to outlast its owner.

He went to make coffee, then came back and plugged the drive into his laptop.

---

The drive was encrypted.

Instead of the usual list of folders and files, the screen showed a password field — a single blinking cursor, waiting. Below it, a short message: *This volume is protected. Enter the passphrase to continue.*

Nael tried the obvious: *tarik*. Incorrect. *kadiri*. Incorrect. *nael*. Incorrect. He tried Tarik's birthday from the passport photocopy — the notary's folder was still on the desk — and his own birthday, and several combinations of both. Nothing.

He sat back and stared at the screen. Then he noticed something he'd missed. There was one file visible outside the encryption — a single text document, small, sitting alone like a note left on a locked door. It was called simply *indice*.

He opened it.

*For the one who received a guide before he could read its words:*
*look again — not at where it points, but at what it says.*
*The answer has always been with you.*

Three lines. No signature. No explanation.

Nael read them again. *A guide before he could read its words.* Something given to him when he was young. Something with words he couldn't read at the time. *Not at where it points, but at what it says.*

He thought of the notebook — pages of symbols he still couldn't decipher. But the clue said *received*, past tense, something he'd been given. And *the answer has always been with you.* Not in the box. With him. Already in his possession.

The compass.

The brass compass with Arabic script. Fourteen directions instead of twelve. A gift from Tarik, wrapped in newspaper, handed to a seven-year-old who couldn't read Arabic and thought the extra markings meant it was broken.

Nael stood up. He crossed the apartment to the bedroom, opened the closet, and pulled down a cardboard box from the top shelf — the one he never unpacked after the last move. Inside: old school notebooks, a broken watch, a tangled set of earphones, and at the bottom, wrapped in a sock, the compass.

It was smaller than he remembered. The brass was tarnished, the hinge stiff. He pried it open. The dial was still intact — fourteen points radiating from the center, each labeled with a character in Arabic calligraphy. He'd looked at it a hundred times as a child without understanding a single word. Now, with enough Arabic to read a restaurant menu and not much more, he studied the markings.

Twelve of them were directions — the standard cardinal and ordinal points, transliterated into Arabic. North. South. East. West. And the intermediaries. But the thirteenth and fourteenth markers, the ones that had made him think the compass was defective, were different. They weren't directions at all.

The thirteenth was a small crescent — decorative, not a letter. But the fourteenth, placed at the very top of the dial where a jeweler might engrave a dedication, was a word. Four Arabic characters, so small he had to hold the compass under the desk lamp to read them.

ابني

He didn't recognize it immediately. He typed the characters into a translation app on his phone, one by one, squinting between the tiny script and the screen.

The result appeared.

*Ibni.* My son.

Nael set the compass down on the desk. He looked at it for a long time. The courtyard was silent. The coffee grew cold.

Tarik had no children. He had a nephew he'd visited once and watched from a distance for seventeen years. And he'd engraved *my son* on a compass he gave to a boy who couldn't read the words.

Nael picked up the laptop and typed into the password field: *ibni*

The drive unlocked.

---

The contents appeared on screen — a set of folders, neatly organized. One was labeled *tarik-open-weights* and contained what looked like an enormous file, nearly forty gigabytes, the kind used to store an artificial intelligence model. Next to it, a folder called *voice*, and a short text file titled *instructions*.

Nael opened the instructions first. His hands were steadier now. He'd earned this.

*For Nael.*

*This is a language model — an artificial intelligence I trained*
*on my personal data: journals, correspondence, recordings,*
*annotations spanning fourteen years. It is not me. It is what*
*I was able to leave behind.*

*To talk to it: download any software that can run local AI*
*models, and load the file from tarik-open-weights.*
*To hear its voice: once the model is running, open the voice*
*folder and launch the program inside.*

*I trust you with this, as I should have trusted you with more.*

*— T.*

The last line was identical to the will. The handwriting had become a typeface, but the voice behind it was the same.

Nael leaned back and looked at the file listing. Forty gigabytes. Fourteen years of a man's inner life — his thoughts, his habits of speech, his memories — compressed into a file that fit on a drive smaller than his thumb. He'd heard of this kind of thing at work, in passing: models trained to mimic a person, to carry their patterns forward after they were gone. He'd always dismissed it as a novelty. An expensive parlor trick. But this was sitting on his desk, left for him by a dead man who'd spent years building it, and the password had been *my son.*

He opened a browser tab and searched for software that could run a local AI model. The first result looked straightforward — a program that loaded model files and let you chat with them through a text window. He downloaded it, pointed it to the massive file on the USB drive, and waited while the progress bar crawled forward.

The model loaded. A text window opened — simple, clean, like a chat screen with no one on the other end. A cursor blinked at the bottom.

Then, without Nael typing anything, the first words appeared. Slowly, letter by letter, as though someone on the other end were choosing each one with care.

```
Hello, my son. I've been waiting for you.
```

*My son.* The same two words engraved on the compass. Not "user." Not a generic greeting. The model knew who he was — who it had been built for.

He typed: `Who are you?`

A pause. The response appeared at the same deliberate pace — not instant, but measured, as though the words were being remembered rather than generated.

```
I am not a who. I am a what.
But the what was built by a who, and the who was your uncle.
```

Nael exhaled slowly through his nose. He typed: `What are you, then?`

```
A model. Trained on personal data — journals, recordings,
correspondence, annotations. Fourteen years of material.
What I know is what he gave me. Nothing more, nothing less.
```

Fourteen years. That was most of his conscious life. He typed: `Tarik built you.`

```
Tarik built me.
```

`Why?`

```
That is a question with layers. Which layer would you like first?
```

Nael studied the screen. The responses had a rhythm to them — a cadence that didn't feel random or mechanical. Not a generic chatbot parroting a script, but something with personality woven into its fabric. It handled French, Arabic, and English without hesitation. It paused in places where a thoughtful person would pause. It was, he realized, the most sophisticated piece of software he had ever seen, and it was running on his laptop from a dead man's USB drive.

He typed: `How do I know this is real? That you're actually based on his data and not just a chatbot with a script?`

```
You were seven when he last visited. Yellow kitchen. Radiators
that clanked. He brought you a compass — brass, with Arabic
script. Fourteen directions, not twelve. You thought it was
broken.
```

Nael's hand went still on the keyboard.

He had never told anyone about the compass. Not the fourteen directions — not until twenty minutes ago, when he'd held the thing under a lamp and read the inscription for the first time. But Tarik had known. Tarik had always known.

He typed: `What else do you know about me?`

```
More than you'd expect. Less than you deserve.
He wrote about you often. More than about anyone else.
```

Something shifted in Nael's chest. A small rearrangement, like furniture moved in a room he'd thought was empty. He ignored it. Typed: `Why the riddles? Just tell me what this is about.`

```
Because he designed me with constraints, my son. I cannot simply
hand you what he left. I can guide. I can explain. I can
remember. But the work is yours.
That was the condition he built into my architecture.
```

`The condition for what?`

A longer pause. Six seconds. The cursor blinked at the end of the last line, waiting.

```
For the inheritance.
Not the four hundred and twelve euros.
The real one.
```

Then, before Nael could type a response, a new line appeared:

```
There is a folder on the drive called voice.
Open the program inside. I would prefer to speak to you properly.
```

Nael stared at the screen. Then he navigated to the USB drive and opened the *voice* folder. Inside was a single program and a note: *Voice model trained on 600+ hours of recorded speech.* He launched it. A small window appeared, connected itself to the running model, and went quiet.

For a moment, nothing. Then his laptop speakers crackled once, and the room filled with a voice.

It was not Tarik's. Not exactly. It was close enough to be wrong in the way a photograph of a dead person is wrong — the proportions correct, the life absent. A baritone with a slight rasp, an accent that hovered between Maghreb French and something more cosmopolitan, more worn. It spoke with the deliberate pacing of a man who had learned to choose his words in several languages.

"I apologize for the theatrics," the voice said. "The text was the foyer. This is the room."

Nael did not move. The coffee had gone cold an hour ago. The courtyard below was silent.

"My son." A pause. "I know this is disorienting. I know you process things by taking them apart. So let me give you something to take apart."

He realized his hands were gripping the edge of the desk. He loosened them. Swallowed.

"What are you?" he said aloud. His voice sounded strange in the apartment — he hadn't spoken since leaving the notary's office.

"The same thing I was thirty seconds ago. A model. But now you can hear what he sounded like." Another pause, as though the voice were selecting from a vast library of phrasings. "He recorded over six hundred hours of speech. I was trained on all of it. I am not him. But I am built from what he left, and what he left was considerable."

"Why me?" he said.

"Because you're the only person he trusted to finish what he started."

"He didn't know me."

"He knew you well enough to write about you for fourteen years. He knew you well enough to build this." The voice softened — not warmer, exactly, but lower, like someone leaning in. "And he knew you well enough to know you'd ask that question first."

A new line appeared on screen:

```
Ask me about the keys.
```

Nael stared at the line. Then he said, "Fine. What keys?"

The voice paused — not from processing delay, Nael thought, but from something designed to feel like consideration. "Tarik created a Bitcoin wallet. A multisignature address — 7-of-7. Do you know what that means?"

"Every key has to sign. All seven. No partial access."

"Correct." If the AI was impressed, its voice didn't show it. "Seven private keys. Each one a twenty-four-word seed phrase. BIP-39 standard. He split them and placed them in seven locations, across several countries."

Nael's mind was already constructing the data model. Seven keys. Twenty-four words each. One hundred and sixty-eight words total, and every single one had to be correct, in sequence. A single transposition and the key wouldn't generate. It was beautiful, in the way certain encryption schemes were beautiful — elegant, merciless, and completely unforgiving.

"How much is in the wallet?" he asked.

"That, I am not permitted to disclose. Not yet. What I can tell you is that it is significant. Enough that he spent the last years of his life designing the system to protect it."

"And you — you know where the keys are?"

"I know where to begin looking for each one. The exact locations are embedded in puzzles he designed. I can provide context, history, clues. But the final steps require you. Physically present. In each place."

"Why can't you just tell me the phrases?"

"Because I don't have them." The voice carried something that sounded almost like regret — a calibrated inflection, but effective. "He partitioned the information. I hold the maps but not the treasure. He was very deliberate about that."

Nael pulled the leather notebook from the desk and flipped through its coded pages. "And this?"

"Supplementary material. Some of it will become legible as you progress. Some of it is personal — his thoughts, his reasoning. He wrote it for you, even if it doesn't look that way yet."

Nael closed the notebook. He stood up, walked to the window, looked down at the courtyard. Someone was smoking. The sky above Paris was the color of dishwater. He turned back to the laptop on his desk, the screen still glowing, the cursor still blinking.

"Seven countries," he said. "You said several. Which ones?"

"The first is the one that matters tonight."

"Tell me all of them."

"No." The refusal was calm, unhurried. "He designed this as a sequence, my son. Each key unlocks the context for the next. If I gave you all seven destinations now, you would plan. You would optimize. You would try to run them in parallel. He knew that about you."

"Is that a criticism?"

"It's a compliment. But the journey is not a system to optimize. It's a path to walk. One step, then the next."

Nael pressed his jaw tight. He recognized the rhetorical maneuver — it was the kind of thing someone says when they want to sound wise and are probably right, which made it worse.

"The first destination," he said.

"Morocco. There is a city called Meknes — the city of a hundred minarets, of walls within walls. That is where you begin. The old medina will guide you, if you know how to read its streets."

"Streets don't talk."

"You sound like your mother."

The sentence landed like a hand on a raw nerve. Nael went still. The ambient noise of the courtyard — the smoker's cough, a television behind a closed window — seemed to recede.

"Don't," he said.

"I will need to," the voice replied, and for the first time its cadence shifted — slower, lower, something almost human pressing through the synthesis. "Because she is part of this story, my son. Not the keys. The other story. The one underneath."

"What other story?"

The silence lasted long enough for Nael to hear his own breathing.

"Tarik didn't leave your family. He was asked to leave. Your mother asked him. She made him promise to stay away — from her, from you, from everything. She had her reasons, and they were not small."

"That's not —" Nael stopped. The denial had been automatic, a reflex, the kind of thing you say before the sentence catches up to what you already, somewhere deep, suspected. His mother had never spoken about Tarik with anger. That was the tell. She spoke about him with the careful, measured blankness of someone who has made a decision and will not revisit it.

"He kept the promise," the AI continued. "For seventeen years. He watched from a distance. He wrote about you — your school, your interests, your first job. Things he learned through channels he never disclosed to me. And he built this." A pause. "All of this. Because the promise was that he would stay away. Not that he would stop caring."

Nael sat down on the edge of the bed. The desk chair was two meters away but the bed was closer and his legs had made the decision before his brain approved it. The laptop sat open on the desk, the screen still glowing, the voice still present in the room like a guest who wouldn't leave.

"Why?" he said, and the word came out smaller than he intended.

"That is not a question I can answer in full. Some of it is in the notebooks. Some of it you will find along the way. And some of it —" the voice paused again, "— you will need to ask her yourself."

Nael said nothing for a long time. The laptop screen dimmed, then brightened, as if the machine were watching him.

"You have your mother's stubbornness," the AI said. "That's why he chose you."

He stood, walked back to the desk, and looked at the screen. The voice had stopped. A single line of text at the bottom:

```
Meknes. Derb Lalla Aïcha, past the fountain that no longer runs.
The door marked with a hand missing its thumb.
When you're ready.
```

Below it, the cursor blinked. Waiting. Not impatient. Not urgent. Just present — the way a door is present when it's been left open.

---

He sat at the same desk at ten past midnight. The coffee had gone cold hours ago. The leather notebook was open to a page of symbols he still couldn't read. The photographs were spread in a row across the desk like tarot cards. The USB drive sat next to the laptop, its machined aluminum catching the screen's glow.

He opened a new browser tab. The old ones were still there — a Jira board, a pull request review, a Slack thread about a deployment that no longer felt like it belonged to his life.

He typed: *Flights Paris to Fez.*

The cheapest option was a Ryanair direct. Fifty-four euros one way. Departing Friday. Fez was the closest airport to Meknes — less than an hour by train.

He looked at the chat window, still open behind the browser. The cursor blinked in the dark.

He looked at the will, still in the notary's folder. *He will know what to do with them.*

He booked the flight.
